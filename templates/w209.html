02 · The hunt

We tested four things everyone believes help

People have plenty of theories about what helps you match. Be more active. Earn more. Fill out your profile. List the right interests. We tested all four, and here is what happened.

Advantage 1 · App usage · Chelsea Fan

Does more time on the app mean more matches?

The thinking is simple: the more you swipe, the more chances you get, so heavy users should match more. We compared match rate across usage levels, from light users to the most active.

Result: flat · ~21–22% across every usage tier
Advantage 2 · Income · Curtis Chau

Do higher-income users match more?

A common assumption is that higher income signals status and gives you an edge. We broke match rate down by income bracket, then split it by gender to check for any hidden differences.

Result: flat · no clear income trend

Advantage 4 · Interests · Sam Kim

Do a user's interests predict how well they match?

The last theory is that your interests say something about how well you'll match, that certain hobbies simply do better than others. We started by ranking all 49 interests by their average match rate.

At first glance this looks like a real ranking, with Stand-up Comedy on top and the rest sliding down from there. But look at the numbers: the whole list sits between about 21% and 23%, so the gaps are tiny.

To make the comparison easier to read, we grouped the 49 interests into 7 broader categories like Arts & Creative and Sports & Outdoors. Each group holds thousands of users, which makes the averages more stable, and then we plotted the full distribution for each one instead of a single average.

Every category lands right on the overall average, and the spread inside each group runs almost the entire range. The interest a user lists barely moves their match rate.

Result: flat · every category lands on 21.7%

Four different "advantages." Four flat lines.
So why does nothing move it?

03 · The reveal

Match rate isn't measuring success. It's measuring its denominator.

For the 90% of users who receive at least 20 likes, the number of actual matches barely moves. It hovers around 15 whether they got 30 likes or 190. Since match rate divides matches by likes, that makes it almost entirely a story about the denominator. Get few likes and your match rate looks high. Get a lot and it looks low, even though you matched with just as many people.

On the left, match rate falls from about 50% down to 8% as likes go up. On the right, the number of actual matches stays flat once users get past the smallest tier (the lowest group is capped only because you can't match with more people than liked you). The metric rewards being unpopular.
04 · What it means

A caution for anyone who builds with metrics

A product manager who optimizes for match rate would be pushing users toward getting fewer likes, which is exactly backwards. The honest way to measure success is raw match count, or the full swipe to like to match funnel. By those measures, nothing in this data predicts who succeeds.

Takeaway for PMs: ratio metrics can quietly flip your incentives. Before you optimize one, check whether it is tracking the outcome you actually care about, or just its denominator.
05 · About this project

Goals, tools and team

Goal: help a dating-app product manager see which user traits drive matching success, and understand why the obvious success metric is misleading. Audience: dating-app product managers. Data: the Kaggle Dating App Behavior dataset, covering 50,000 synthetic users.

Tools: we built the analysis and charts in Tableau, published them to Tableau Public, and embedded them here with the Tableau Embedding API. The site runs on Flask and is hosted on Vercel. We chose Tableau because it let us iterate quickly and add cohort filters without writing code.
Iteration: we refined the interest view from 49 cramped bars down to 7 readable categories, fixed axes that made tiny differences look big, and most importantly, shifted the whole project from "which advantage wins" to exposing the match-rate illusion once the data pointed us there.
A note on the data: this is a synthetic dataset, so the demographic and behavioral features are uniform by design and carry no real signal. That is why every "advantage" comes out flat. We treat the project as a working scaffold: the same views and pipeline would surface real differences if pointed at live data, while the match-rate illusion is a structural point about the metric that holds either way.
