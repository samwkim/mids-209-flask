03 · The reveal

Match rate says more about your likes than your success

Here is the catch. Almost everyone ends up with about the same number of matches, around 15, no matter how many likes they get. So your match rate really just comes down to how many likes you received. If only a few people liked you, those matches look like a huge success rate. If hundreds liked you, the exact same matches look like a low one, even though you matched with just as many people. In other words, getting fewer likes actually makes your match rate look better, which is backwards.

So a product manager who optimizes for match rate would be pushing users toward getting fewer likes, which is exactly backwards. Match rate probably is not a metric to trust on its own. As a next step, we want to look into other measures of success, like the raw number of matches or the full swipe-to-like-to-match funnel, and test whether any of them tell a clearer story than match rate does.

Takeaway for PMs: a metric can quietly point you the wrong way. Before you optimize for one, make sure it is really tracking the success you care about.

05 · In conclusion

Nothing predicts success here, and that is still useful

Across every trait we grouped by and every outcome we measured, nothing in this dataset predicts who succeeds on the app. A lot of that comes down to the data being synthetic, so there was never a real signal waiting to be found.

The value isn’t the answer, it’s the setup. Everything on this page, the metric check, the interactive explorer, and the funnel view, is a ready-made scaffold. Pointed at real dating-app data, a product manager could quickly see which levers actually move matches, which groups of users behave differently, and whether the metric they report on is tracking real success or just an illusion. We built the lens. Real data would fill in the picture.
